⚡ Live Event — June 18, 2026: Trump Announces Intel-Apple Foundry Deal

President Trump announced today that Apple has agreed to work with Intel to design and build chips domestically using Intel's 18A process technology. Neither Apple nor Intel has issued formal confirmation. Apple will use 18A for entry-level M-series chips starting in 2027, estimated at 15–20 million units annually — roughly $500M–$1B in annual revenue by 2028. Intel stock is up ~9% on the news. We are initiating coverage on the day of this announcement, and our analysis reflects both the strategic significance and the current-valuation context.

Five Key Tensions

  1. Apple Deal Is a Historic Endorsement vs. 15–20M Units Is Economically Immaterial. Apple choosing Intel 18A for any production is the most important external foundry validation in Intel's history. But 15–20M units for entry-level M-series represents ~5–8% of Apple's total chip production and ~$0.5–1.0B revenue — less than 0.2% of Intel's $590B market cap on an annual basis.
  2. DCAI +22% YoY "CPU Renaissance" vs. AMD Epyc Continues to Take Structural Share. Xeon 6 "Granite Rapids" is genuinely competitive. But AMD Epyc has won the architectural debate and holds ~34% of new server CPU deployments. Intel's gains are real but cyclical (inventory replenishment + AI inference demand) not structural.
  3. NVIDIA + SoftBank + US Government as Strategic Anchors vs. FCF −$3.1B TTM. A $5B NVIDIA investment, $2B SoftBank, and $8.9B CHIPS Act funding change Intel's downside calculus entirely. But the company burned $3.1B in free cash flow in the last twelve months and carries $45B in debt.
  4. Lip-Bu Tan's "Speed-of-Light" Execution vs. Six Consecutive Beats Already In the Price. Tan has delivered 6 straight beats since taking over in March 2025 — the clearest CEO-quality signal in semis. But the stock has already tripled. The beats are pricing in his continued execution, not rewarding it.
  5. 18A Process Ahead of Expectations vs. Industry-Standard Yields Not Until 2027. Yields currently 65–75%, "ahead of expectations." Apple's entry validates the node. But TSMC N2 is already in volume production for Apple's premium chips — Intel's 18A is a second-source for low-end M-series, not a TSMC displacement.

Executive Summary

Intel is executing the most credible industrial turnaround in semiconductor history. Under Lip-Bu Tan, the company has delivered six consecutive revenue and EPS beats, restructured from 12 management layers to 5, shed 15%+ of its workforce, secured $8.9B from the US government, attracted $5B from NVIDIA and $2B from SoftBank, and just — on the day of this initiation — had its foundry ambitions validated by an Apple chip deal announced directly by the President of the United States.

And yet: at $121 per share and a ~$590 billion market cap, the stock is trading at over 100x our FY2026 Non-GAAP EPS estimate of ~$1.10 and at ~74x our FY2027 estimate of ~$1.65. Free cash flow remains deeply negative (−$3.1B TTM). Debt stands at $45B. The 18A process node won't reach industry-standard yields until 2027. AMD will not give back data center CPU share easily.

The turnaround is real. The valuation reflects it — and then some. We rate Intel Hold / PT $120. We would become buyers on a pullback to $95–105, or on a confirmed, scaled Apple deal with formal 18A yield disclosure that exceeds current expectations. Our bull case ($220) requires Intel Foundry to become a profitable external manufacturing business serving multiple hyperscalers. Our bear case ($60) requires FCF deterioration and AMD continuation of share gains through 2027.

Business Overview

Intel operates three primary reportable segments:

  • Client Computing Group (CCG) — ~56% of Q1 revenue ($7.7B): PC and laptop processors (Core Ultra, previous generation). The PC market is in a multi-year refresh cycle driven by Windows 11 AI hardware requirements. CCG is Intel's largest revenue segment but its most mature. Competitive pressure comes primarily from AMD (Ryzen AI) and Apple Silicon (M-series) rather than traditional server rivals.
  • Data Center & AI (DCAI) — ~37% of Q1 revenue ($5.1B): Server CPUs (Xeon 6 "Granite Rapids") and AI accelerators (Gaudi 3). This is the growth engine. Xeon 6 is recapturing inference workload share as enterprises discover that agentic AI workflows are as CPU-intensive as GPU-intensive. Gaudi 3 AI-specific revenue exceeded $750M in Q1 2026, nearly double from a year ago. ASIC revenue (custom accelerators) crossed a $1B+ annual run rate.
  • Intel Foundry — ~40% of Q1 revenue ($5.4B): Primarily internal manufacturing of Intel's own chips (Intel 3, Intel 18A). External revenue is minimal today but is the highest-optionality segment in the business. The Apple deal, if formally confirmed, would represent the first major external customer on 18A. NVIDIA's $5B strategic investment is widely interpreted as potential future foundry business. Process nodes: Intel 3 (currently in production), 18A (ramping with yields 65–75%), 14A (PDK finalized, customer decisions H2 2026).

CEO Lip-Bu Tan (took over March 2025) has restructured the company from 12 management layers to 5, cut workforce by 15% to ~75,000 employees, adopted a "bad news first / 24-hour escalation" culture, and recruited senior talent from Qualcomm and Arm to lead DCAI. Six consecutive quarters of beating expectations is his scorecard.

The Foundry Thesis — Intel's 10-Year Bet

Intel is attempting to do what no other company has ever done: convert a captive manufacturing operation into a commercial foundry while simultaneously competing with TSMC in process leadership. The bull case on Intel is almost entirely a foundry story.

18A Process Node

  • Current yields: 65–75% — workable for low-volume production, not fully competitive with TSMC N2 for high-volume manufacturing
  • Target: Intel's CFO guided yields to "desired cost level by end of 2026," industry-standard in 2027
  • Apple deal: 15–20M units of entry-level M-series starting 2027. Apple will still use TSMC for ~80% of chip production including all high-performance iPhone/Mac chips
  • 18A-P: Next iteration of 18A; management citing "inbound interest" from external customers

14A Process Node

  • PDK (process design kit) finalized; considered industry-standard by independent reviewers
  • Hyperscaler customers expected to make 14A design-in decisions in H2 2026 and H1 2027
  • If 1–2 hyperscalers commit to 14A, the foundry thesis becomes a multi-year revenue story at meaningful scale

The NVIDIA Read-Through

NVIDIA's $5B strategic investment in Intel is the most underappreciated element of the thesis. NVIDIA has extraordinary TSMC concentration risk — all of its H-series and B-series GPUs are manufactured at TSMC. A $5B commitment to Intel is consistent with NVIDIA wanting a second credible foundry option. If NVIDIA begins ordering from Intel Foundry — even at 20% of volume — it would be transformative for Intel's foundry business at a scale that dwarfs the Apple deal.

AI Strategy — Gaudi 3, Xeon 6, and the CPU Revival

Intel's AI thesis operates on two distinct tracks:

Track 1: Xeon 6 for AI Inference (Existing Install Base)

The shift from AI training (GPU-intensive) to AI inference and agentic AI workflows (CPU-intensive) is Intel's most immediate growth catalyst. Every enterprise deploying AI agents needs both GPU capacity and CPU capacity — Xeon 6 "Granite Rapids" is capturing the CPU side of that equation. Intel CEO Lip-Bu Tan described Q1 demand as driven by "agentic CPU demand," and management expects this trend to sustain DCAI growth above 20% YoY through FY2026.

Track 2: Gaudi 3 for AI Training (New Market)

Gaudi 3 is Intel's GPU-competitive AI accelerator. It is not competing with NVIDIA H200/B200 for the highest-performance training workloads — it is competing for cost-sensitive enterprise AI training where open ecosystem and lower licensing costs are priorities. Gaudi 3 revenue exceeded $750M in Q1 (from ~$400M a year ago) and is deployed in select AWS and IBM Cloud clusters. This is real adoption, not vaporware — but Intel is not threatening NVIDIA's datacenter GPU dominance in any near-term scenario.

Intel's AI opportunity is about two things: (1) Xeon 6 capturing inference CPU share in AI-augmented enterprise workloads — a CPU renaissance narrative that is already printing in the numbers; and (2) Gaudi 3 as a cost-effective NVIDIA alternative for non-premium training workloads. Track 1 is happening now. Track 2 is real but small. Neither threatens NVIDIA. Both are incrementally positive for Intel.

What You Need to Believe

Bull Beliefs (to go long above $121)

  1. The Apple deal formally confirms, scales, and leads to 14A production for premium M-series. If Apple follows the 15–20M unit 18A trial with a 14A commitment for premium M-series chips (similar volumes to TSMC N3), Intel Foundry's external revenue could exceed $5B annually by 2029. Falsifiable: watch for formal Apple/Intel PR, 8-K disclosure of material foundry agreement, or Q2 FY2026 earnings call commentary on Apple relationship.
  2. NVIDIA converts its $5B investment into actual foundry orders on 14A. NVIDIA currently has 100% of its GPU manufacturing at TSMC. Any disclosed Intel Foundry design win for a future-generation GPU would re-rate the entire foundry thesis. Falsifiable: NVIDIA earnings call commentary or 8-K foundry agreement by end of 2026.
  3. DCAI sustains 20%+ YoY growth into FY2027 as agentic AI drives sustained CPU demand. Q1 2026 DCAI +22% was driven by Xeon 6 ramp and AI inference. If this continues into FY2027 (H2 2026 and 2027 prints), Intel re-establishes data center CPU revenue leadership. Falsifiable: DCAI growth rate in Q2–Q4 FY2026 earnings prints.
  4. FCF turns positive in FY2027 as restructuring savings flow through and foundry loss narrows. Intel is targeting $16B OpEx in 2026 vs. $17B in 2025. If DCAI and foundry revenue grow faster than costs, the company reaches positive FCF territory. Falsifiable: FCF in quarterly prints; management FCF guidance at Q4 FY2026.

Bull PT: $220 — FY2027E Non-GAAP EPS $1.65 × 90x (foundry-validated premium multiple) + foundry strategic option value (~$30/share for 1–2 hyperscaler commitments on 14A)

Bear Beliefs (why to not own above $121)

  1. Apple deal is permanently limited at 15–20M units because 18A never scales to TSMC-competitive yields. If yields stay sub-70% through 2027, Apple cannot use 18A for anything beyond a politically motivated, sub-scale contract. Revenue stays $500M–1B and the TSMC displacement narrative collapses. Falsifiable: 18A yield data from management guidance and 14A customer decision timelines.
  2. AMD continues to take 2–3pp of server CPU share per quarter. AMD Epyc's architectural advantages in memory bandwidth and power efficiency are structural, not cyclical. Intel's Xeon 6 gains may be inventory-cycle driven; next-gen Epyc could re-accelerate AMD share gains in H2 2026. Falsifiable: AMD earnings call server CPU commentary; IDC market share data quarterly.
  3. FCF remains negative through 2027 as 18A ramp costs exceed revenue benefit. Intel is spending capex to build out Ohia (Ohio fab), Magdeburg (Germany), and ramp Hillsboro (18A). If external customers don't commit, Intel is running a $10B+/year capex program for internal use only at deeply sub-scale economics. Falsifiable: quarterly FCF prints and capex trajectory.
  4. The strategic investor coalition is financial — not operational. NVIDIA invested $5B as a hedge, not as an intent to order. SoftBank invested for financial returns. If neither converts to foundry orders, the investment coalition is P&L-irrelevant. Falsifiable: NVIDIA and SoftBank foundry order disclosures (or absence) through 2027.

Bear PT: $60 — FY2027E Non-GAAP EPS $0.80 (FCF negative, foundry dilutive) × 45x (turnaround premium but no foundry premium) + debt discount = ~$60
