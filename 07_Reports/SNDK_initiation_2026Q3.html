Sandisk is experiencing a NAND supercycle of a magnitude the industry has not seen since the 2021 DRAM spike — and SNDK is the purest expression of it in US equity markets. The company spun off from Western Digital on February 24, 2025 with a depressed balance sheet and a 22.5% gross margin. Eighteen months later, gross margins hit 78.4%, FCF is $3.0B per quarter, the balance sheet has zero debt and $3.7B net cash, and Q4 guidance midpoint implies $31.50 in non-GAAP EPS — a single quarter larger than most analysts' full-year estimates from six months ago.

The catalyst behind this transformation is twofold: (1) a NAND pricing supercycle driven by AI infrastructure buildout, with ASPs surging an estimated 55–75% over the last year; and (2) Sandisk's "New Business Model" (NBM) — a series of multi-year supply agreements with hyperscale customers that convert a historically spot-market-driven commodity business into contracted, recurring revenue with floor-and-ceiling pricing and financial guarantees if purchase obligations are not met. Five NBM agreements have been signed, representing a $42B+ multi-year backlog.

We initiate coverage at Hold with a $2,000 price target, representing 34.7x our derived FY2026E non-GAAP EPS of $62.82 less an 8% discount to reflect execution risk on the NBM thesis. We are not skeptical of the fundamental story — the AI storage demand thesis is real and the NBM framework is genuinely novel for the NAND industry. But the stock at $2,182 (up ~5,500% since its February 2025 spinoff price near $38) is pricing in a very optimistic scenario: that EPS will continue growing in FY2027 and the NBM framework will durably suppress the cyclicality that has historically devastated memory margins. Our DCF implies ~$380 in a base case and ~$1,000 in a bull case — the gap between DCF and market price is a measure of the option value the market assigns to the NBM narrative. At these levels, we need more certainty before upgrading.

Our view: The bull case is possible but prices in the best-case scenario without adequately compensating for the bear case. The NBM agreements are real ($42B backlog, $511mm in deferred revenue on the balance sheet) but they have not been tested in a down-cycle. If NAND ASPs normalize 30–40% from current levels — which is not the base case but happened in 2019, 2022, and 2023 — then the "floor-ceiling" pricing on NBM deals still exposes SNDK to revenue declines. Meanwhile, Samsung and SK Hynix, who collectively control ~52% of NAND supply, have historically been willing to sacrifice margins to gain share. The variant here is not that the thesis is wrong — it's that the risk/reward at $2,182 is unfavorable (+37% bull / −82% bear) and we need to see another quarter of NBM execution before committing at these prices.

2. Company Overview

Business Description

Sandisk Corporation (NASDAQ: SNDK) is a pure-play NAND flash memory manufacturer spun off from Western Digital Corporation on February 24, 2025. The company designs, manufactures, and sells NAND flash memory products under the Sandisk brand for datacenter, mobile, PC, gaming, and consumer applications. SNDK operates through three end-market segments: Datacenter (enterprise NVMe SSDs, QLC NAND for AI workloads), Edge (client SSDs, UFS embedded NAND for smartphones, industrial), and Consumer (SD cards, USB drives, retail SSDs).

SNDK's manufacturing backbone is a joint venture with Kioxia Corporation (formerly Toshiba Memory) in Japan — one of the world's largest NAND manufacturing partnerships. The JV operates fabs in Yokkaichi and Kitakami, Japan, and produces BiCS FLASH (vertical NAND) jointly. The JV has been extended through 2034 and currently manufactures on BiCS8 (218-layer), with BiCS10 (300+ layers) scheduled to begin production in 2026. SNDK has committed $1.17B in manufacturing payments to the JV and sources substantially all of its bit output through this structure.

The New Business Model (NBM)

The NBM is the most important structural development in SNDK's history. Historically, NAND manufacturers sold product into spot markets with ASPs that oscillated violently with supply/demand balance. The NBM converts this into long-duration customer agreements with three key features: (1) multi-year committed volumes from hyperscale customers; (2) floor-ceiling pricing so neither side is exposed to extreme market moves; and (3) financial guarantees backed by prepayments and third-party instruments if purchase obligations are not met. As of Q3 FY2026, five NBM agreements are signed representing over $42B in multi-year backlog. The $511mm in contract liabilities on the Q3 balance sheet (current $323mm + non-current $188mm) represents cash already received from customers as deposits — locked-in future revenue recognition.

Kioxia Alliance and Competition

The Kioxia relationship is both SNDK's greatest manufacturing asset and a structural tension. The JV provides shared R&D and capex costs that give SNDK manufacturing scale it could not achieve as a standalone $19B-revenue company. However, Kioxia is also SNDK's direct competitor in enterprise SSDs, including the AI datacenter segment where both companies are aggressively recruiting hyperscaler customers. TrendForce (January 2026) noted that the two companies are "balancing alliance and rivalry" — collaborating on manufacturing while competing for the same hyperscaler wins. The JV extension through 2034 locks SNDK into this dynamic for the foreseeable future.

What the data shows: The sequential gross margin expansion from 22.5% → 29.7% → 50.9% → 78.4% in four quarters is parabolic and almost certainly reflects both extraordinary NAND ASP increases AND the early contribution of NBM agreements (which carry higher-than-spot margins). The 78.4% GAAP gross margin is software-like for a hardware manufacturer — it is not a typo.

Why it matters: At a 78.4% gross margin with $448mm in quarterly OpEx, SNDK is generating $71-cent operating income for every dollar of revenue. The resulting FCF ($3.0B in a single quarter) has enabled the company to retire $1.9B of debt in nine months while accumulating $3.7B in net cash.

What to watch: Q4 guidance midpoint implies 80% GM, suggesting FURTHER expansion. The question is whether BiCS8's cost efficiency and NBM contract pricing structures are sustainable, or whether raw material costs and ASP normalization will mean-revert margins toward the 40–50% range in FY2027.

What could go wrong: The 78.4% Q3 GM may include favorable timing of NBM revenue recognition (deferred revenue released at favorable contract prices). If the underlying NAND bit-price environment softens by 20–30%, GAAP GM could compress 600–1,000bps quickly. Management's guidance credibility (see Section 6) is extremely strong — the track record is massive upside surprises — but Q4's guided 79–81% GM makes any Q4 guidance miss particularly notable.

Datacenter ($1,467mm, +645% YoY): The AI storage story. Enterprise NVMe SSDs for training dataset storage and inference KV cache. SNDK's NBM agreements are concentrated here — the five signed deals are hyperscaler-focused and appear to be primarily datacenter-segment revenue. The 645% YoY growth rate is extraordinary but starts from a tiny base ($197mm). At 24.7% of Q3 revenue, Datacenter is not yet the dominant segment; the unit economics are presumably the best in the portfolio (NBM pricing with floor-ceiling guarantees).

Edge ($3,663mm, +295% YoY, 61.6% of Q3 revenue): This is the actual revenue driver. Edge encompasses client SSDs for PCs and laptops, UFS (Universal Flash Storage) embedded NAND for smartphones, and industrial applications. The AI PC upgrade cycle (every AI PC needs high-speed local NAND for model caching) and smartphone replacement cycle are tailwinds. The +118% QoQ in Edge is particularly notable — this suggests broad-based demand acceleration, not just datacenter. The risk is that this segment is more price-sensitive and more exposed to consumer spending cycles than Datacenter.

Consumer ($820mm, −10% QoQ, +44% YoY): SD cards, USB drives, retail SSDs. The QoQ decline is typical seasonal softness coming off a holiday quarter. At 13.8% of revenue this segment is strategically de-emphasizing as SNDK prioritizes higher-margin datacenter and enterprise business. Consumer NAND margins are structurally lowest of the three segments.

The segment mix shift is the real story: Datacenter went from 11.6% of revenue in Q3 FY2025 to 24.7% in Q3 FY2026. If this continues (and the NBM agreements suggest it should), datacenter could be 35–40% of revenue by end of FY2027 — which fundamentally changes the margin profile and multiple the business deserves. This is the core of the bull case. The bear case is that the Edge segment (60%+ of revenue) is more cyclical than it appears and a PC/smartphone cycle turn could pressure both volume and pricing simultaneously.

7. What You Need to Believe

🟢 Bull Case (to go long at $2,182)

  1. The NBM framework has fundamentally broken the memory cycle. The $42B backlog with financial guarantees creates a revenue floor that did not exist before. Even if NAND spot prices drop 30%, SNDK's contracted revenue stream means earnings don't crater the way they did in 2019 or 2022. Evidence: $511mm in deferred NBM deposits already on balance sheet; management confirms 5 agreements with more being signed. Falsifiable: if any NBM customer invokes a force majeure or walks from a contract, the structural narrative collapses.
  2. AI storage demand is a multi-year, supply-constrained secular tailwind. By 2026, an estimated 1-in-5 NAND bits are consumed for AI applications. Training large models requires petabytes of high-speed NAND storage. Inference requires low-latency KV cache storage. This is not cyclical demand but capacity-driven demand that grows with every new GPU installation. Evidence: Datacenter revenue +645% YoY to $1.47B in a single quarter. Falsifiable: if datacenter revenue growth decelerates below 50% YoY for two consecutive quarters before reaching $3B/quarter.
  3. EPS compounds toward $80–100+ in FY2027 as more NBM agreements close. With 5 agreements and $42B backlog, additional agreements would expand the backlog geometrically. Q4E already guides to $31.50/quarter. If a 6th and 7th NBM agreement sign before FY2027 begins, Q1 FY2027 could be $35–40. FY2027E EPS of $80–100 puts the stock at 22–27x forward earnings — reasonable for a high-visibility, high-margin business. Falsifiable: if no new NBM agreements are announced in FY2027.
  4. BiCS10 (300+ layer) cost reduction drives margin durability into down-cycle. BiCS10's 300+ layer architecture produces significantly more bits per wafer than BiCS8 (218-layer). If BiCS10 ramps successfully in 2026–2027, SNDK can sustain 65–70%+ gross margins even if ASPs normalize 20%. Evidence: JV capex committed; $1.17B in manufacturing payments to Kioxia JV for capacity. Falsifiable: if BiCS10 ramp is delayed more than 2 quarters from 2026 schedule.
Bull PT: $3,000 FY2027E EPS $80 × 37.5x = $3,000 (+37%)

🔴 Bear Case (for caution / shorts)

  1. 78.4% gross margins are not a new normal — they are a supercycle peak. NAND gross margins in 2019: 15–20%. In 2022: turned negative for some manufacturers. In 2023: near zero. The history of NAND is that the industry builds capacity until margins collapse. Samsung and SK Hynix can flood the market the same way they did before. The NBM floor-ceiling pricing helps but doesn't eliminate this risk — the "ceiling" in the ceiling pricing means SNDK gets capped on upside too. Evidence: Samsung still controls 32.3% of market and has not publicly committed to supply restraint. Falsifiable: if Samsung announces capex cuts, bear case weakens significantly.
  2. 61.6% of revenue (Edge) is NOT AI — it's smartphones and PCs. The bull narrative focuses on datacenter, but Edge is where the revenue is. Smartphone NAND demand is tied to handset upgrade cycles (flattening), not AI capex budgets. A global consumer slowdown or smartphone inventory digestion could pressure Edge revenue 20–30%, driving total revenue materially below Q4 guide trajectory. Evidence: Consumer segment already declined −10% QoQ in Q3 (early softness signal?). Falsifiable: if Edge sustains >80% YoY growth into FY2027 Q2.
  3. The Kioxia JV is a structural handcuff, not a strategic asset in a down cycle. SNDK is committed to $1.17B in manufacturing payments to the JV through 2034. In a severe down-cycle, these become fixed costs that cannot be shed. Kioxia is also a direct competitor for the same hyperscaler NBM agreements — creating a conflict of interest that may constrain SNDK's ability to sign customers that overlap with Kioxia's own NBM outreach. Evidence: TrendForce noted "balancing alliance and rivalry" as the dominant dynamic between the two. Falsifiable: if SNDK signs NBM agreements specifically excluding Kioxia as a competing bidder.
  4. The DCF doesn't support the current price under any realistic scenario. Our base-case DCF (14% WACC, 2.5% terminal growth, FCF declining from $7.9B to $4.5B trough by FY2029) implies ~$380/share. Even a bull-case DCF (12% WACC, optimistic FCF trajectory) implies ~$1,000. The gap between DCF value and market price ($2,182) implies the market is either pricing at very low discount rates OR betting on FCF growing beyond $30B within a decade. That's the NBM thesis fully playing out — a legitimate upside but a very high bar. At current prices, the bull case upside (+37%) does not compensate for the bear case downside (−82%).
Bear PT: $400 Cycle normalization, EPS $25–30, 15x = $375–450 (−82%)

11. Capital Allocation

SNDK entered its post-spinoff life with a leveraged balance sheet ($1.83B LT debt at FY2025 end) and zero cash generation at the margin level. The velocity of the balance sheet transformation is as striking as the income statement improvement: the company has gone from net debt of $348mm (FY2025 end) to net cash of $3,735mm (Q3 FY2026) in three quarters — a $4.1B swing entirely generated by operating cash flow.

Current capital allocation priorities (inferred from balance sheet and management commentary):

  • Debt retirement (complete): $1.9B in debt retired in Q1–Q3 FY2026. SNDK is now debt-free.
  • JV manufacturing commitments (ongoing): $1.17B in committed Kioxia JV payments through 2034. These are relatively modest (~$130mm/year equivalent) against current FCF run-rates.
  • Share buyback (not yet announced): Management referenced repurchase authorization discussions on the Q3 call. With $3.7B+ net cash and $3B+ quarterly FCF, buybacks are the most likely next capital allocation step. At current prices, buybacks would be accretive to EPS but represent aggressive capital return at an expensive multiple.
  • M&A (not indicated): No acquisition discussions disclosed. Given the NBM strategy (organic customer agreements), inorganic growth is not a near-term priority.
  • Dividend (not yet): No dividend declared. Unlikely until the company establishes 2+ years of consistent FCF generation.
Capital allocation risk: SNDK has never operated as a standalone company through a full NAND down-cycle. In a scenario where FCF drops from $3B/quarter to $500mm/quarter (as it did effectively in 2022–2023 when the sector was underwater), the current leverage-free balance sheet is the primary safety valve. Using the cash buffer for buybacks at $2,182/share — near all-time highs — would be poor capital allocation discipline. We would want to see management articulate a through-cycle capital return framework before being comfortable with a large buyback authorization.
