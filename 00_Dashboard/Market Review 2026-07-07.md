## 1. What is actually happening

Three distinct things are being priced at once, and conflating them is how mistakes get made this week:

**(a) A sentiment/rotation unwind of the AI trade, not a demand break.** The proximate triggers: Meta's July 2 announcement that it will resell excess AI compute (read as both a demand-deceleration signal and a direct competitive threat to neoclouds), Michael Burry's disclosed MU short plus "insiders dumping Micron at the highest rate since 2010" headlines, and Samsung's July 7 flash — the largest quarterly operating profit ever recorded by a tech company (OP 89.4T KRW, +56% QoQ; revenue 171T KRW vs consensus 155–165T) — being sold −6.9% in Seoul as "priced in." Today's tape is textbook rotation: Dow +200, Nasdaq −0.7%, SMH −5.8% intraday. Money is leaving semis, not equities.

**(b) The fundamental data is going the other way.** Samsung's flash affirms the WFE demand backdrop ([[Catalyst_Reaction_SAMSUNG_FLASH_2026-07-07]]): the 110T KRW 2026 capex plan (+128% YoY) is undisturbed, HBM4 mass production is ramping, and DRAM/NAND ASPs are rising. No coverage name has fired a bear-case *thesis* trigger. The bears' argument is valuation and sustainability-of-spend, not current-quarter demand — which is exactly the argument that was wrong in the June 23 selloff and got resolved by the MU print.

**(c) Macro noise on top:** record May trade deficit, Fed-hike chatter (Evercore sees no move this year), Strait of Hormuz tanker attacks, and a NATO summit. None of it is AI-infrastructure-specific; all of it raises the multiple-compression risk while positioning unwinds.

**Working diagnosis: (a) is the driver, (b) says don't de-risk the thesis, (c) says don't add indiscriminately either.** The distinction that matters: *prices* are breaking bear levels; *theses* are not. The framework already has an answer for that — bear-price breaches force re-underwrites, not reflexive exits.
