# Gas Turbine Backlog Pricing Power

> The global heavy-duty gas turbine market is an oligopoly of three manufacturers — GE Vernova, Siemens Energy, and Mitsubishi Power — all of whom have sold out production capacity through 2028-2029. New-price slot reservations are being booked at 10-20 points above existing backlog, meaning margin expansion is structurally locked in for years regardless of new order pace.

---

## The Core Thesis

When supply is physically constrained and cannot grow quickly, pricing power accrues entirely to sellers. That is the situation in gas turbines today.

Three manufacturers control effectively all supply of heavy-duty gas turbines above 100 MW: GE Vernova (largest installed base, HA-class world efficiency record), Siemens Energy (SGT-9000HL and derivatives), and Mitsubishi Power (JAC/M701JAC class). New entrants cannot replicate this position in less than a decade — turbine manufacturing requires unique metallurgical capabilities, decades of aerodynamic learning, a global service infrastructure, and an installed base large enough to justify R&D costs. The barriers are effectively permanent on any relevant investment horizon.

What changed in 2024-2026 is the demand side. AI data centers require dispatchable, high-capacity-factor electricity. The only technology meeting this requirement at scale today — without years of permitting and grid connection delays — is natural gas turbines. Hyperscalers and utilities competing for the same limited manufacturing slots has produced the rarest of market conditions: a seller's market in industrial capital equipment.

---

## The Mechanism: Slot Reservations and Pricing Uplift

Gas turbine delivery timelines run 18-36 months from order to commissioning. Once a manufacturer's production schedule is full, new customers must make non-binding "slot reservations" — paying a deposit to hold a future production slot, with pricing negotiated at the time of formal booking.

The critical dynamic: existing backlog was priced in 2022-2024, when gas turbine demand was below current levels and pricing reflected competitive market conditions. New reservations are being priced in 2025-2026, under full sold-out conditions. The delta is 10-20 percentage points of dollar-per-kilowatt pricing above existing backlog — disclosed directly by GE Vernova management on the Q1'26 earnings call (April 22, 2026).

**The math:** If GEV's legacy backlog averages $400/kW and new reservations are being booked at $480-$480/kW (+20%), then as each old-price order converts to revenue and is replaced by a new-price order, realized margins expand structurally — not cyclically.

This is the mechanism behind GEV's raised 2026 EBITDA margin guidance (12-14%, up from 11-13%), the Power segment's expanding margins, and the basis for analyst arguments that consensus EPS estimates are "still too low."

## Evidence

**Q1'26 data points (GE Vernova, April 22, 2026):**
- Gas turbine backlog + slot reservations hit 100 GW — three quarters ahead of original year-end 2026 target
- Management immediately raised target to ≥110 GW by year-end
- Q2'26 quarter-to-date (through April 22): more power equipment orders in $ value than all of Q1
- Pricing on first-half 2026 orders: "10-20 points higher on a dollar per kW basis than our Q4'25 orders"
- Orders with ~90 distinct customers in 24 countries — demand is not dependent on any single customer type or geography

**Siemens Energy corroborating data:**
- SMNEY also disclosed sold-out production capacity through 2028-2029
- Both companies are charging customers for slot reservation deposits, a practice not seen in the prior cycle

**Customer behavior confirms scarcity:**
- Utilities booking turbine slots 3-5 years ahead of project construction
- Hyperscalers signing "framework agreements" for multi-unit, multi-year gas turbine supply — unprecedented
- Approximately 20% of GEV gas turbine contracts now explicitly tied to data center customers (up from essentially zero in 2022)

## Duration and Risk Assessment

**How long does the pricing power last?**

The constraints are supply-side, not demand-side. New turbine manufacturing capacity requires 3-5 years to plan, build, and qualify. Even if GEV or Siemens Energy began expanding production today, meaningful new capacity would not be available until 2029-2030 at the earliest. This means the pricing power window runs through at least 2028, with the inflection likely visible in financial results through 2030 as the backlog converts.

**What breaks the thesis?**

1. *AI capex normalization* — if hyperscalers sharply reduce data center investment, new order pace moderates. However, the existing 100 GW+ backlog continues converting at new-price economics regardless; only the 2028-2030 vintage would be affected.

2. *Competing technology displacement* — nothing currently displaces heavy-duty gas turbines for behind-the-meter data center baseload at scale. Battery storage, SMRs, and distributed generation are complements, not substitutes, on a 3-5 year horizon.

3. *Permitting and project delays* — if data center developers cannot get their plants permitted and operational, turbine orders could be deferred or cancelled. GEV's CEO flagged growing state-level resistance to data centers as a concern (Bernstein conference, May 27, 2026). This is the primary near-term risk to new order pace.

4. *Customer bankruptcy or counterparty risk* — unlikely given hyperscalers are the counterparties; high-quality credit.
