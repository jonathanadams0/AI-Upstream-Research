# Uranium Supply-Demand Inflection

> The uranium market is experiencing its first genuine supply-demand inflection in 15 years. Nuclear power is the only 24/7 zero-carbon generation source at commercial scale — the precise combination hyperscalers and governments need for AI baseload. The hyperscaler PPA wave has created a new class of nuclear demand that wasn't in any forecast 3 years ago, while uranium supply remains structurally constrained by mining lead times and geopolitical concentration.

---

## The Core Thesis

Uranium's demand-supply imbalance has three distinct legs:

**Leg 1: Existing reactor lifetime extensions.** Utilities with existing nuclear fleets (VST, CEG, NRG, EDF, E.ON) are extending reactor licenses by 20-40 years, securing decades of uranium fuel demand. Each license extension requires a long-term uranium enrichment contract. TMI restart (CEG), Palisades restart (Michigan), Perry/Davis-Besse/Beaver Valley (VST) — all extended, all needing uranium.

**Leg 2: AI hyperscaler PPAs driving nuclear re-pricing.** When Microsoft signs a 20-year PPA for TMI's output at premium prices, it validates nuclear economics for the next generation of reactor investment. Each hyperscaler nuclear PPA signals to utilities and governments that nuclear can attract premium pricing — incentivizing additional license extensions and new builds that require more uranium.

**Leg 3: New construction — SMRs and conventional.** South Korea's APR1400 program (building 10 units), UAE's Barakah expansion, Poland's Westinghouse AP1000 program, UK's Hinkley Point C + proposed Sizewell C, GEV's BWRX-300 (Canada + US pipeline). Each new reactor requires 40+ years of uranium fuel supply secured through long-term contracts.

## Supply Constraints

**Kazakhstan concentration:** Kazakhstan (primarily Kazatomprom) produces ~40% of global uranium. Supply is constrained by: (1) sulfuric acid shortages needed for in-situ recovery, (2) rail logistics bottlenecks for export, (3) geopolitical uncertainty given Russia's influence.

**Western mine development lead time:** Building a new uranium mine takes 7-15 years from discovery to production. No amount of demand growth can add new supply in less than that timeframe. Cameco's Cigar Lake and McArthur River are the world's highest-grade, most cost-effective uranium deposits — and Cameco is sold out.

**Russian Uranium Prohibition Act (2024):** The US prohibited imports of Russian enriched uranium. Russia had supplied ~35% of US enriched uranium. This has created an enrichment capacity bottleneck in the US and EU that won't be resolved for several years.

**HALEU (High-Assay Low-Enriched Uranium):** Advanced SMRs (GEV BWRX-300, Kairos FHR, Oklo Aurora) require HALEU — uranium enriched to 5-20% U-235 rather than the conventional 3-5%. HALEU production capacity barely exists outside Russia. This is a distinct bottleneck from conventional uranium supply.

The long-term contract price (what matters for Cameco's earnings) has been rising consistently and is now well above the price needed for most new mine development — though new mines still face 7-15 year lead times regardless of price.

## The HALEU Wildcard

HALEU supply is the most acute near-term bottleneck for SMR commercialization. Without HALEU, BWRX-300, Aurora (Oklo), and most advanced reactor designs cannot be fueled. Current US HALEU production capacity is negligible; the Department of Energy has programs to develop capacity but they are years away.

**Investment implication:** Any company that develops commercial HALEU enrichment capacity could be extraordinarily valuable. Centrus Energy (LEU) is the primary public-market HALEU play — not currently in coverage but worth monitoring.

## Risks

1. **Kazatomprom supply normalization:** If Kazakhstan resolves its sulfuric acid and logistics constraints, supply could surprise to the upside
2. **China nuclear slowdown:** China is the largest single contributor to new reactor demand; any slowdown impacts long-term uranium demand forecasts
3. **Reactor delays:** US and EU nuclear new-build programs have historically been plagued by cost overruns and delays; SMR timelines are uncertain
4. **Political risk:** Nuclear remains politically sensitive; changing government priorities could affect license extensions
