# Critical Minerals & Mining — Sector Primer

---

## 1. Industry Overview

Critical minerals are the physical foundation of both the AI infrastructure buildout and the energy transition. These materials are characterized by extreme supply inelasticity — new mines take 10–15 years from discovery to first production, meaning demand shocks hit price rather than supply. This makes mining equities high-beta plays on the secular themes driving the portfolio.

Four commodities matter most for the AI/energy buildout:

1. **Copper** — every MW of data center, every km of transmission line, every electric motor requires copper. ~65 tonnes per MW of data center capacity; ~4 tonnes per EV; ~8 tonnes per wind MW. S&P estimates data center copper demand alone could reach 300,000+ tonnes/year by 2030 — roughly 10% of current global production.
2. **Uranium** — nuclear reactor restarts (TMI, Kewaunee, Palisades) and long-term SMR deployments for data center baseload are creating a structural uranium demand step-up. Utilities are now signing 10-15 year enrichment contracts at prices well above spot.
3. **Rare earths (NdPr)** — neodymium-praseodymium magnets power wind turbine generators, EV motors, and data center cooling systems. China controls ~85% of global rare earth processing — supply chain decoupling is a strategic priority for US/EU governments.
4. **Silver** — solar panels (5% silver intensity per MW), semiconductor contacts, high-end electronics. Less AI-pure but adjacent to the energy transition buildout.

## 2. AI/Energy Linkage

**Copper:**
- Data centers are the single fastest-growing source of copper demand globally
- A 100 MW hyperscaler campus requires ~6,500 tonnes of copper in transformers, cables, busbars, cooling systems, and data infrastructure
- Grid upgrade to accommodate data center load requires additional copper in transmission and distribution
- S&P Global Commodity Insights estimates AI-driven data center copper demand adds ~1-2% to global consumption annually through 2030

**Uranium:**
- Hyperscaler PPAs for nuclear (Microsoft/TMI via CEG, Meta/Vistra PJM nuclear, AWS/Comanche Peak) have demonstrated that tech companies will pay premium prices for guaranteed clean baseload power
- Each nuclear PPA signed at above-spot prices provides implicit floor for long-term uranium enrichment contracts
- SMR development programs (GEV's BWRX-300, NuScale, Kairos) create additional long-run uranium demand tail

**Rare Earths:**
- Wind turbine direct-drive generators (offshore especially) require NdPr permanent magnets — ~600 kg per turbine
- EV motors: ~1 kg NdPr per motor; ~70M EVs/year by 2030 = ~70,000 tonnes NdPr demand
- US government has designated rare earths a critical national security material; MP Materials is the only US producer at scale
- China's export restrictions on rare earth processing technology (announced 2023, expanding) are accelerating Western supply chain build-out

**Supply inelasticity (universal):**
- Copper mine development: 7–10 years from discovery to production
- Uranium: 5–7 years for brownfield, 10–15 years for greenfield
- Rare earth separation facilities: 5–8 years to build (US); China has decades of process optimization

## 3. Value Chain

```
Exploration → Development → Mining → Refining/Processing → Fabrication → End User
                                             ↑
                              Critical Choke Point
                    (esp. for rare earths — 85% processed in China)
                    (uranium enrichment — concentrated in Russia/US/EU)
```

**Copper value chain:**
```
Porphyry deposit → Open-pit mining (FCX, SCCO) → Concentrating → Smelting → Refined copper → Fabrication
```

**Uranium value chain:**
```
Mining (CCJ, UUUU) → Conversion (ConverDyn, Orano) → Enrichment (Urenco, USEC) → Fuel fabrication → Reactor
```

**Rare earth value chain:**
```
Mining (MP Materials, UUUU) → Separation (MP Materials' SOP facility) → NdPr metal → Magnet manufacturing → End product
```

## 6. Commodity Deep Dives

### Copper: The AI Metal

Copper is the highest-confidence AI/energy transition beneficiary because demand is not speculative — it is physics. Copper conducts electricity; every watt of data center power and every km of transmission line requires it.

**Demand drivers:**
- Data centers: +300,000 t/yr by 2030 (~10% of current global production)
- EV motors + charging infrastructure: +1.5M t/yr by 2030 vs. 2022
- Grid transmission/distribution upgrades: +1M t/yr by 2030
- Total AI+energy transition demand addition: ~3–4% of current global supply annually

**Supply constraints:**
- Global copper mine supply growing at ~2-3% annually; cannot keep pace with AI+EV+grid demand
- Major porphyry deposits are depleting — Grasberg, Escondida ore grades declining
- New greenfield development (Chile, Peru, DRC) facing permitting, water, and political risk
- S&P base case: structural copper deficit developing by 2025-2028; price needed to incentivize new supply = $12,000-15,000/tonne (vs. current ~$9,000/tonne)

**FCX vs. SCCO:**
- FCX: larger absolute scale, Grasberg mine in Indonesia (political risk, but world-class deposit), also has gold exposure
- SCCO: lower cost structure (~$1.20/lb vs. FCX's ~$1.80/lb), higher margins, Mexico/Peru focus, family-controlled (minority shareholder risk)

### Uranium: The AI Baseload Fuel

Nuclear power is the only 24/7 zero-carbon generation source available at scale — the precise combination hyperscalers need for data center baseload. This is driving the nuclear power renaissance and with it, uranium demand.

**Demand step-up:**
- US reactor restarts: Three Mile Island (CEG/Microsoft), Palisades (under evaluation)
- Extended licenses: Comanche Peak (VST/AWS), Perry/Davis-Besse/Beaver Valley (VST/Meta), through 2040-2060
- New builds: SMRs from GEV (BWRX-300 Ontario, Canada; Texas partnership with Blue Energy)
- Global: Japan restarts, UK Hinkley Point C, France EPR2 program, South Korea APR1400

**Supply constraints:**
- Kazakhstan (Kazatomprom) dominates global supply; production constrained by sulfuric acid and logistics
- Canada (Cameco's Cigar Lake, McArthur River): world-highest-grade uranium; Cameco sold out through 2028+
- US domestic production essentially zero; White Mesa Mill (UUUU) ramping but small
- Russian Uranium Prohibition Act (2024): US utilities barred from importing Russian enriched uranium → US enrichment capacity bottleneck

**Key Cameco thesis:** CCJ is the best-positioned Western uranium producer for the nuclear renaissance because (a) it has the highest-quality Canadian assets, (b) it co-owns Westinghouse Nuclear Services (via Brookfield JV) providing both fuel cycle and reactor service exposure, and (c) it is selling forward at long-term contracted prices substantially above historical.

### Rare Earths: The Decoupling Trade

China controls ~85% of global rare earth separation and processing. US, EU, Japan, and Australia are investing in domestic supply chains to reduce dependence — a process that takes 5-10 years and creates significant opportunity for non-Chinese producers.

**MP Materials thesis:**
- Only US rare earth miner AND processor at scale (Mountain Pass mine + separation plant in CA)
- Building US magnet manufacturing capability (Fort Worth, TX facility)
- If fully vertically integrated by 2025-2026: mine → separated NdPr → magnet → motor OEM
- Largest risk: NdPr prices set in China; volatile; can pressure MP margins if Chinese supply surges

**UUUU thesis:**
- White Mesa Mill in Utah = only operating conventional uranium AND rare earth mill in US
- Dual exposure: uranium (benefiting from nuclear renaissance) + rare earths (benefiting from supply chain decoupling)
- Small-cap with significant optionality; binary outcomes more likely than large-caps

**Valuation debate:** Mining equities are inherently cyclical — the question is always "where are we in the commodity cycle?" The AI/energy transition argument is that this is a structural, multi-decade demand shift, not a cyclical inventory restocking. If correct, traditional cyclical valuation (EV/EBITDA below mid-cycle) substantially understates fair value.

## 8. Key Theses

- **Copper Structural Deficit** — demand from AI data centers + EVs + grid upgrades is growing 3-4% annually; supply growing at 2-3%; structural deficit emerges 2025-2028; price needed to incentivize new supply = $12,000-15,000/t
- **Uranium Supply-Demand Inflection** — nuclear renaissance driven by hyperscaler PPAs, reactor restarts, SMR development; Western uranium supply constrained; Cameco positioned as dominant supplier
- **Rare Earth Decoupling from China** — bipartisan US policy + EU Critical Raw Materials Act creating structural demand for non-Chinese supply; MP Materials is the primary US beneficiary

---

## 9. Risks

1. **Commodity price volatility** — mining equities have high beta to underlying commodity prices; a recession scenario can compress copper/uranium/NdPr prices 30-50%
2. **Jurisdictional risk** — major copper deposits in Chile, Peru, DRC, Indonesia face political risk, royalty increases, and nationalization risk
3. **Permitting** — US mining permitting timelines are structurally disadvantaged; a new US copper mine can take 10-20 years to permit
4. **Substitution** — high copper prices invite substitution (aluminum in some cable applications); fiber optic substitutes for copper in communications
5. **China policy wildcard** — China can flood rare earth markets at subsidized prices to undercut Western producers (as it did in 2011-2012)
6. **Recession risk** — industrial metals are pro-cyclical; AI-driven demand floor provides support but does not eliminate recession sensitivity
