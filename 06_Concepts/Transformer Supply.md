# Transformer Supply — The Grid Infrastructure Bottleneck

## What It Is

Large power transformers (LPTs) — the steel-cored, copper-wound devices that step voltage up or down between transmission and distribution — are in severe global shortage. Lead times for utility-scale transformers have extended from the historical norm of ~50 weeks to 120-180+ weeks (2.5-3.5 years) as of 2025-2026.

This shortage is directly relevant to data center construction timelines and to the valuation of companies in the [[Power-Gen-Grid]] sector.

## Why Transformers Are Scarce

**1. Manufacturing complexity and geographic concentration**
Large power transformers are custom-engineered, precision-manufactured equipment. Each unit requires:
- Grain-oriented electrical steel (GOES) for the core — highly specialized, few global producers
- High-purity copper windings — 10-50+ tonnes per transformer
- Expert assembly in specialized facilities with large overhead cranes

US domestic transformer manufacturing capacity is limited. The majority of large transformers consumed in the US are imported — primarily from South Korea, Mexico, and Canada. Only a handful of US plants manufacture units above 100 MVA.

**2. Demand acceleration**
Transformer demand has surged from multiple simultaneous drivers:
- AI data centers (each large campus requires multiple 100+ MVA transformers)
- Grid interconnection for renewable energy projects (solar and wind farms each require step-up transformers)
- Utility grid replacement cycle (US transformer fleet is aging; average age exceeds 40 years)
- Electrification of industrial loads (EV charging, heat pumps, industrial electrification)

**3. Supply chain constraints**
- **GOES:** Global GOES production is concentrated in Japan (Nippon Steel, JFE Steel), South Korea, Germany, and China. GOES capacity takes years to expand; new rolling mills require $500M-$1B+ investment.
- **Skilled labor:** Transformer winding and assembly requires experienced tradespeople; training cycles are 3-5 years.
- **Factory capacity:** Building new transformer manufacturing plants requires 3-5 years and $200M+ in capital.

## Lead Time Data

| Transformer Type | Normal Lead Time | 2025-2026 Lead Time |
|---|---|---|
| Distribution (< 5 MVA) | 16-26 weeks | 50-70 weeks |
| Power (5-100 MVA) | 40-60 weeks | 80-120 weeks |
| Large power (100-500 MVA) | 50-80 weeks | 120-180+ weeks |
| Extra-large (500+ MVA, HVDC) | 80-100 weeks | 150-200+ weeks |

Source: Edison Electric Institute surveys, utility procurement reports, industry trade press.

## Why Prolec Was Strategic for GEV

GEV paid for Prolec GE's remaining 50% (completing full ownership in February 2026) precisely because transformer supply constraints are real, multi-year, and the Prolec facility cannot be quickly replicated by competitors.

Prolec's San Luis Potosí plant has:
- Domestic North American manufacturing (preferred by US utilities and data center customers for supply chain security)
- ~$4B of backlog at time of acquisition
- 20%+ EBITDA margins at current pricing

With transformer lead times at 2-3.5 years and hyperscalers needing transformers *now*, GEV can charge premium pricing for expedited delivery from Prolec's US-proximate facility. This is the core of the Electrification segment's margin expansion story.

## When Does It Normalize?

The transformer shortage will ease as new capacity comes online, but the timeline is longer than most investors assume:
- **Short term (2025-2026):** Continued shortage; pricing power at maximum
- **Medium term (2027-2028):** New manufacturing investments (GEV Prolec expansion, SPX Transformers, ABB capacity adds) begin producing; some lead time improvement
- **Longer term (2029-2030):** Supply-demand more balanced; pricing power moderates but structural demand growth from grid electrification prevents full normalization
