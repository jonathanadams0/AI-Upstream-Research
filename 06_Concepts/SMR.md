# SMR — Small Modular Reactor

## What It Is

A Small Modular Reactor (SMR) is a nuclear fission reactor with an electrical output typically under 300 MW (vs. 1,000-1,600 MW for conventional large nuclear plants). The "modular" component refers to factory fabrication of standardized components that are then assembled on-site — in theory reducing construction time, cost, and risk compared to bespoke large-plant construction.

SMRs are relevant to this portfolio because hyperscalers are actively investing in SMR development and deployment as a long-term solution to their baseload power needs. Every major tech company — Microsoft, Google, Amazon, Meta — has announced some form of SMR involvement.

## Why Hyperscalers Want SMRs

AI data centers need power with three properties simultaneously: **dispatchable** (available 24/7 on demand), **zero-carbon** (net-zero commitments), and **scalable** (multiple GW per campus). Large conventional nuclear meets all three but cannot be built quickly enough (~15 years from site selection to operation in the US). Renewables are zero-carbon but not dispatchable. Natural gas is dispatchable but not zero-carbon.

SMRs are the promised solution: smaller, faster-to-deploy nuclear that could be sited closer to data centers. The promise is 5-10 year deployment timelines vs. 15+ years for large conventional plants.

## BWRX-300 — Most Relevant to This Portfolio

The BWRX-300 is the most commercially advanced SMR with direct portfolio implications:

- **Canada (Ontario Power Generation, Darlington site):** First BWRX-300 under construction. Expected commercial operation ~2030. This is the Western world's first SMR in construction.
- **Texas (Blue Energy partnership with GEV, announced May 2026):** 2.5 GW project pairing BWRX-300 SMRs with 7HA.02 gas turbines for data center power. FID targeted 2027, SMR delivery 2032. The gas turbines bridge the gap until SMRs come online.
- **US NRC regulatory review:** NRC reviewing BWRX-300 Standard Design Approval application. A favorable decision is needed before US commercial deployment.

**Why BWRX-300 matters for GEV:** GEV Hitachi Nuclear Energy co-owns the BWRX-300 technology with Hitachi-GE Nuclear Energy. Each BWRX-300 deployment adds to GEV's nuclear services revenue stream alongside gas turbines. The Blue Energy deal also deploys GEV gas turbines as bridge power, meaning GEV wins twice.

## Timeline Reality Check

The "5-10 year deployment" promise for SMRs has been consistently delayed in practice:

- NuScale's lead project (Carbon Free Power Project) was cancelled in 2023 after cost estimates tripled
- Regulatory timelines at the NRC remain lengthy (3-5 years for standard design approval)
- Supply chain for SMR components is largely unproven at scale
- Financing structures for pre-revenue nuclear projects are complex

**Practical implication:** SMRs are real and advancing, but they are not displacing gas turbines or large conventional nuclear in this decade. The near-term (2025-2030) power need for data centers is being met by gas turbines (GEV, Siemens Energy), nuclear PPAs on existing plants (CEG, VST), and grid expansion (ETN, HUBB, PWR). SMRs are a 2030s+ story for new capacity.

## Uranium Connection

Each SMR requires enriched uranium fuel. BWRX-300 uses High-Assay Low-Enriched Uranium (HALEU) — a fuel type with limited current production capacity. If BWRX-300 deployment scales, HALEU supply becomes a new uranium enrichment bottleneck that benefits [[CCJ]] (via Westinghouse fuel services) and potentially [[UUUU]] (which has been exploring HALEU-capable enrichment).
