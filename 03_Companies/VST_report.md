## Executive Summary

Vistra is the largest competitive power generator in the United States, with 38,700 MW of diversified generation capacity — gas, nuclear, coal, solar, and battery storage — and a retail electricity business serving 4.3 million customers primarily in Texas. The stock has underperformed sharply over the past year (−13%) against a backdrop of strong fundamentals: Q1'26 adj. EBITDA of $1.49B beat estimates by more than 2x, the company reaffirmed its 2026 Adj. EBITDA guidance of $6.8–7.6B and FCFbG guidance of $3.9–4.7B, signed a landmark 20-year nuclear PPA with Meta for 2,609 MW, and acquired investment-grade credit ratings from both S&P and Fitch. The Cogentrix natural gas acquisition (5,500 MW, targeting H2 2026 close) will add ~14% to generation capacity.

**We initiate with a Buy and a $230 price target.** At ~$154, VST trades at approximately 9.7x 2026E EV/EBITDA — a steep discount to both regulated utilities (~15x) and pure-play nuclear peers like Constellation (~16x). The discount reflects leverage (~$19B net debt), commodity exposure, and the complexity of the retail integration, but does not adequately credit the locked-in value from multi-decade nuclear PPAs, the Cogentrix capacity addition, or the structural step-up in earnings power driven by data center load growth in ERCOT and PJM. The sell-side agrees: 20 Buy ratings, 2 Holds, zero Sells, mean PT $228.

**Three things that matter most:** (1) ERCOT summer 2026 — VST is massively exposed to Texas power prices; a hot summer drives beat potential while mild weather is a headwind (Q1'26 was one of the mildest in Texas history, yet they still beat handily); (2) Cogentrix close and integration — 5,500 MW of gas-fired capacity at attractive entry cost increases FCF power; (3) Nuclear PPA ramp — the Meta deal delivers escalating contracted revenue through 2034, structurally reducing commodity earnings volatility in the PJM segment.

## 1. Investment Thesis

**VST is the highest-quality integrated power generator in the U.S. at the wrong multiple.**

The investment case for Vistra rests on three structural arguments that converge at a moment when the stock trades well below its intrinsic value.

**Thesis Point 1: The nuclear fleet is being re-priced from commodity to contracted, permanently.**

Vistra owns 6,400 MW of nuclear generation across five plants: Comanche Peak (Texas/ERCOT), and Perry, Davis-Besse, Beaver Valley, and Braidwood (PJM). These plants were historically valued as commodity generators — their output priced into spot power markets, making them sensitive to gas prices and weather. The Energy Harbor acquisition (2024) doubled Vistra's nuclear fleet and has been followed by a rapid transformation: hyperscalers are signing 20-year power purchase agreements at fixed prices for guaranteed baseload power.

The Meta PPA (January 2026) is the defining event: 2,609 MW of nuclear power from three PJM plants (Perry, Davis-Besse, Beaver Valley) on 20-year terms, plus 433 MW of uprates making it the largest nuclear uprate supported by a corporate customer in U.S. history. The AWS PPA (September 2025) added 1,200 MW from Comanche Peak on a 20-year term. Together, these contracts have transformed ~3,800 MW — more than half of Vistra's nuclear fleet — from commodity to contracted. The contracted value is substantially above the market's current implied earnings multiple, suggesting the market has not yet repriced the portfolio.

**Thesis Point 2: ERCOT structural load growth + market design = durable earnings power.**

Vistra's Texas segment is the largest contributor to earnings and the most exposed to weather and commodity volatility. The bear thesis on VST has historically been ERCOT downside risk — mild weather, low gas prices, or unfavorable capacity auctions could significantly reduce Texas segment earnings. This risk is real but has been partially offset by structural change: data center load growth in ERCOT (driven by AI and the absence of zoning restrictions in Texas) is absorbing capacity that would previously have pushed prices down. ERCOT load growth has reaccelerated to 3-5% annually — well above the ~1% historical trend — driven by data centers in the DFW and Houston corridors. Vistra's retail business (~4.3 million customers) provides a natural hedge: when wholesale prices spike, retail margins compress; when prices fall, retail captures the benefit.

**Thesis Point 3: Cogentrix + investment grade = dual FCF and cost-of-capital optionality.**

The Cogentrix acquisition (5,500 MW of natural gas generation across the eastern U.S., target close H2 2026) adds scale at an entry price that management characterizes as attracting mid-teens levered returns — consistent with their disciplined capital allocation threshold. This immediately adds FCF power while diversifying geographic exposure beyond ERCOT. Simultaneously, dual investment-grade ratings (S&P 2025, Fitch May 2026) unlock senior unsecured debt markets, reduce cost of capital, and eliminate restrictive covenant packages from legacy secured debt. The rating upgrade triggered "fallaway provisions" releasing liens on assets — a structural deleveraging milestone that institutional lenders and fixed-income investors value highly, and that should facilitate refinancing at substantially lower coupon rates over the next several years.

## 2. Business Overview

Vistra reports across six segments, but the economic drivers are simpler than the structure implies.

**Texas (ERCOT — ~45% of EBITDA)**
VST's largest segment. Includes ~15,000 MW of combined gas and coal generation in ERCOT, plus Comanche Peak nuclear (2,300 MW), plus the retail TXU Energy brand. The Texas segment's EBITDA is highly weather-sensitive (Q1'26 was exceptionally mild, yet adj. EBITDA tracked well toward guidance), but the structural ERCOT load growth from data centers provides a floor that did not exist in prior cycles.

**East (PJM/other regulated markets — ~35% of EBITDA)**
Post-Energy Harbor: includes Perry (1,240 MW), Davis-Besse (894 MW), Beaver Valley (1,710 MW), and Braidwood (2,386 MW nuclear), plus gas generation in PJM and other markets. The Meta PPA covers Perry, Davis-Besse, and Beaver Valley starting 2026-2027. PJM capacity auction results have been consistently favorable for dispatchable nuclear, and the cleared capacity provides earnings floor.

**Retail (integrated with segments above)**
Vistra's retail brands (TXU Energy, Dynegy, Homefield, Ambit, others) serve 4.3 million residential, commercial, and industrial customers across 20 states. Retail provides a natural hedge against wholesale price volatility and creates customer stickiness through service relationships that pure-play generators lack. Retail is also Vistra's avenue to corporate PPA relationships at the retail (front-of-meter) level for mid-market data center customers.

**West, Sunset, Asset Closure**
Smaller segments. Sunset and Asset Closure represent legacy coal assets being retired on a defined timeline — these segments generate minimal EBITDA and are winding down, reducing environmental liability over time.

**Revenue model:** Vistra earns through (a) merchant power sales at spot and forward prices, (b) capacity payments from RTO capacity auctions, (c) contracted PPA revenues (growing rapidly), and (d) retail electricity margin. The diversification across all four sources is a strategic differentiator versus pure-play generators.

## 3. AI Exposure

**Classification: Direct**
**AI-linked revenue: ~3,800 MW of nuclear capacity contracted to hyperscalers on 20-year terms; additional discussions ongoing**

Vistra's AI exposure is among the most direct of any publicly traded power generator:

- **Meta PPA (Jan 2026):** 2,609 MW (2,176 MW operating + 433 MW uprates) from Perry, Davis-Besse, and Beaver Valley nuclear plants. 20-year term. Delivery begins late 2026, full operating delivery by end 2027. Uprate delivery 2031-2034. Largest corporate nuclear uprate commitment in U.S. history.
- **AWS PPA (Sep 2025):** 1,200 MW from Comanche Peak nuclear. 20-year term (with option to extend 20 more years). Delivery beginning Q4 2027, ramping to full capacity by 2032. Perry license renewed by NRC through 2046, supporting the long-dated contract structure.

Combined, these two deals lock in ~3,800 MW at contracted prices for 20+ years — pricing substantially above historical merchant power prices in both ERCOT and PJM. The contracts reduce commodity earnings volatility and provide institutional-quality, investment-grade-backed cash flows that the market has not yet fully re-rated.

**Durability:** Exceptional. Nuclear power's combination of zero-carbon output, 24/7/365 availability, and 20-60 year plant lifetimes makes it the ideal hyperscaler baseload solution. No technology available today can replicate this combination. The demand from data centers for firm, clean, dispatchable baseload is structural and growing — every new AI cluster requires guaranteed power at 90%+ capacity factor.

**Additional opportunities:** Management has disclosed "high levels of interest" from hyperscalers in co-location at nuclear sites, bridge power solutions (using Vistra's gas fleet as transition capacity before nuclear comes online), and front-of-meter retail deals. The Cogentrix acquisition adds 5,500 MW of gas capacity that could serve as "bridge power" for data center campuses under construction before their permanent nuclear or renewable supply comes online.

### Balance Sheet

VST's balance sheet is the primary bear argument: $19.2B net debt against $52B market cap = ~3.7x net debt/adj. EBITDA on 2025 figures. This is high, and it reflects the legacy of the Energy Harbor acquisition financed with debt.

However, leverage is declining: (a) Cogentrix is being financed with new notes but adds significant EBITDA immediately; (b) dual investment-grade ratings create refinancing optionality at lower rates; (c) FCFbG of $4.3B midpoint in 2026 allows $2-3B per year of accelerated debt paydown after dividends and buybacks; (d) the contracted nuclear PPA revenues are effectively investment-grade cash flows that ratings agencies credit differently than merchant power revenues — this structural shift is part of why both S&P and Fitch upgraded.

### DuPont / ROIC

Simplified at scale: with $7.2B Adj. EBITDA on ~$40B total assets, asset turns imply a 17-18% pre-D&A return on assets — strong for a capital-intensive utility. After D&A ($2.9B annually), ROIC is lower but still positive and improving as contracted revenues replace merchant volatility.

Only major generator with both (a) the largest nuclear fleet acquired through Energy Harbor + Comanche Peak, (b) a fully integrated retail business at scale, and (c) active hyperscaler PPA relationships at two different grid regions (ERCOT + PJM).

**Moat:**

## 7. Risks

**1. ERCOT weather/commodity risk.** Q1'26 was one of the mildest in Texas history — the fleet "delivered strong performance" but EBITDA in Q1 was only $1.49B (an annualized ~$6B run-rate, below the $7.2B midpoint). A repeat of mild 2026 summer in ERCOT is the single largest downside risk. Natural gas price spikes can help (VST is a net power seller, benefits from high prices) or hurt (fuel cost exposure on unhedged generation).

**2. Leverage.** $19.2B net debt is high. If EBITDA disappoints, the leverage ratio spikes and re-rating probability falls. However, the investment-grade rating and FCFbG guidance of $4.3B midpoint support manageable deleveraging at current earnings levels.

**3. Cogentrix integration risk.** 5,500 MW of gas-fired capacity across multiple geographies introduces integration execution risk. The entry cost appears attractive, but cost overruns or operational issues would delay the FCF accretion thesis.

**4. Regulatory / PJM capacity markets.** Future PJM capacity auction outcomes are uncertain. The cleared price in recent auctions has been favorable for dispatchable generation (nuclear, gas), but regulatory changes to capacity market design could alter the economics.

**5. Nuclear operating risk.** All nuclear plants face unplanned outage risk. An extended outage at Perry or Davis-Besse — now under contract to Meta — could trigger force majeure provisions and damage the contracted revenue stream.

**6. Retail margin compression.** In competitive retail markets, wholesale price spikes can compress retail margins if VST cannot pass through costs quickly. The Texas regulatory environment is favorable, but retail margin pressure is a persistent risk.

## 9. Investment Conclusion

Vistra is the most attractive large-cap power generator available today on a risk/reward basis. The stock has underperformed sharply while the fundamental story has dramatically improved: dual investment-grade credit, landmark nuclear PPAs with Meta and AWS, a major acquisition that adds 14% to generation capacity, and Q1'26 results that beat by more than 2x on EPS. The market has not re-rated the stock — it sits at 9.7x NTM EV/EBITDA vs. nuclear peers at 16x and the sell-side consensus at $228.

The bear cases are real — ERCOT weather, leverage, Cogentrix integration — but are adequately priced at current levels. The bull case involves a partial re-rating toward regulated or contracted utility peers as the Meta and AWS deals begin delivering revenue. At $230 PT (11x 2027E EBITDA), we do not require a full re-rating to nuclear peer multiples — just modest recognition that the contracted revenue fraction is growing and the business is becoming structurally less risky.

**For portfolio construction:** Buy at current levels. The risk/reward at $154 with $230 base PT (49% upside) and $295 bull case (92% upside) offers a compelling asymmetry. The bear case ($105) implies a dramatic ERCOT miss plus execution failure — possible but not the base case for a company that just posted Q1 adj. EBITDA of $1.49B in one of the mildest Texas winters on record.
