## 2. Thesis (3 Bullets)

1. **HBM4 is the next leg, and Micron is ahead of schedule.** HBM4 12-high is ramping 2x faster than HBM3E 12-high did; Micron has already shipped >$1B in HBM4 revenue as of Q3 FY2026. HBM3E + HBM4 are fully booked through CY2027, with demand extending into 2028. The competitive concern (SK Hynix dominance) is fading as all three suppliers are now qualified and production-ramping.

2. **The revenue step-change is structural, not cyclical.** Q3 FY2026 revenue was $41.46B (+346% YoY), driven by data center ($25B+) and NAND ($9.9B, +361% YoY). Q4 guide of $50B at 86% gross margin signals this is not a beat-and-retreat quarter — it's a new floor. 16 Strategic Customer Agreements (~$22B) have been signed, locking future revenue with hyperscalers.

3. **Idaho CHIPS Act + customer lock-in defends the moat.** The $40B+ federal investment in Idaho creates the only large-scale domestic HBM manufacturing base. Combined with signed SCAs, Micron now has multi-year revenue visibility and a government backstop on capital risk — reducing the cyclicality risk that historically plagued the memory sector.

## 4. Key Risks

1. **SK Hynix / Samsung competitive response** — both ramping HBM4; risk is now more about market share than qualification (all three qualified). Diminished but not zero.
2. **Memory cyclicality** — even with AI floor and SCAs, DRAM can overbuild. The SCAs reduce but don't eliminate this risk.
3. **China restriction re-emergence** — Micron lost China sales in 2023; escalating US-China trade war could recur. Invalidation trigger: 8-K disclosing new restriction.
4. **Idaho fab execution** — large capital projects carry schedule risk; delays would shift HBM capacity timelines.
5. **Gross margin ceiling** — 86% guide is exceptional; a sustained margin decline would signal ASP erosion before volumes catch up.
