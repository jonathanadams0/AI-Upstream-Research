## 2. Thesis (3 bullets)

1. **Irreplaceable nuclear fleet + hyperscaler PPAs = decades of contracted earnings.** CEG operates ~22 GW of nuclear across 14 plants (21 units) — 2.5x larger than any US competitor. The 20-year Microsoft PPA (Three Mile Island restart) and 20-year Meta PPA (Clinton) establish nuclear as hyperscaler baseload infrastructure, not a commodity power source. These contracts lock in premium pricing with creditworthy counterparties through the 2040s.

2. **Calpine transforms the earnings profile.** The $26.6B acquisition (closed Jan 2026) adds ~33 GW of gas and geothermal, creating a ~55-60 GW diversified fleet. Management guides >20% EPS accretion in 2026 and >$2B/year incremental FCF. The gas fleet provides dispatchable capacity that complements nuclear baseload and positions CEG for additional data center co-location deals.

3. **Nuclear uprate/restart pipeline provides multi-year earnings growth.** 1.6 GW of incremental nuclear capacity by 2040: TMI/Crane restart (835 MW, targeting 2027 — one year ahead of original schedule), Braidwood/Byron uprates (~135 MW by 2026-2029), Clinton uprate (30 MW by 2029). Each MW of nuclear capacity is worth more today than 18 months ago because of PPA repricing.

## 4. Business Overview

### Segments (2025 Revenue: $25.5B; pro forma with Calpine ~$35-38B)

**Pre-Calpine (CEG standalone):**
- **Nuclear Generation** (~22 GW): 14 plants, 21 units across IL, PA, MD, NY, NJ, TX. Produced ~183 TWh of zero-emissions electricity in 2025 at 94.7% fleet capacity factor.
- **Commercial/Retail:** Integrated retail electricity supply operation (legacy Exelon retail business).

**Post-Calpine (added Jan 2026):**
- **Natural Gas** (~33 GW): Largest US natural gas fleet. CCGT and peaker plants across key markets.
- **Geothermal** (The Geysers): World's largest geothermal complex in Northern California. Clean, baseload generation.
- **Combined fleet:** ~55-60 GW total, producing ~10% of US clean energy.

### Geography
- Nuclear concentrated in PJM (IL, PA, MD), NYISO (NY), and ERCOT (TX minority stake at South Texas)
- Calpine gas fleet adds significant ERCOT, CAISO, and PJM presence
- Geysers geothermal in California

### Key Customers
- **Hyperscalers:** Microsoft (TMI PPA), Meta (Clinton PPA), CyrusOne
- **US Government:** Federal procurement deal
- **Utilities/Wholesale:** PJM capacity market, NYISO, ERCOT bilateral contracts
- **C&I/Retail:** Commercial and industrial customers through integrated retail business

### Revenue Model
- **Contracted/PPA revenue:** Long-term hyperscaler PPAs (20-year), utility bilateral contracts
- **Merchant/wholesale:** Capacity and energy market revenue (PJM, NYISO, ERCOT) — nuclear is baseload price-taker with near-zero marginal cost
- **Nuclear PTC:** $15/MWh (inflation-indexed) via IRA — acts as earnings floor
- **Capacity payments:** PJM capacity auctions provide fixed revenue layer
- **Retail margin:** C&I supply contracts

## 5. Industry & Competitive Position

See sector primer: [[Power-Gen-Grid]]

- **Market position:**
  - Largest US nuclear operator (~22 GW) — 2.5x the next-largest (Duke Energy ~8.6 GW regulated)
  - Post-Calpine: largest US natural gas fleet (~33 GW)
  - World's largest geothermal operator (The Geysers)
  - Combined ~55-60 GW fleet produces ~10% of US clean energy

- **Moat:**
  - **Irreplaceable assets:** No new nuclear plants are being built in the US at this cost (Vogtle 3&4 came in at ~$35B for 2.2 GW). CEG's fleet was built decades ago at a fraction of replacement cost.
  - **Regulatory barriers:** NRC licensing, 40-80 year plant lives, multi-year restart processes. TMI restart alone is $1.6B and 3+ years.
  - **Zero-carbon baseload monopoly:** Nuclear is the only generation source that is simultaneously dispatchable, zero-carbon, and high-capacity-factor (>94%). This is exactly what hyperscalers need and cannot get from renewables + storage.
  - **Switching costs:** 20-year PPAs with hyperscalers = locked-in relationships
  - **Scale:** Fleet size enables operational efficiency, shared maintenance resources, and bargaining power with fuel suppliers

- **Key competitors:** [[VST]] (Vistra — Comanche Peak + Energy Harbor nuclear, ~6 GW), NextEra (FPL nuclear ~3.5 GW), Duke (regulated nuclear), [[NRG]] (gas-heavy IPP)

- **Porter's 5:**
  - Rivalry: **Low** in nuclear (oligopoly of existing fleet owners; no new builds); **Medium** in gas (more competitive but consolidating via M&A)
  - New entrants: **Very low** for nuclear; **Low** for large-scale gas (capital + permitting barriers)
  - Substitutes: **Low near-term** — renewables + storage cannot replicate 24/7 baseload at hyperscaler scale this decade; **Medium long-term** as storage costs decline
  - Buyer power: **Currently weak** — hyperscalers need clean baseload and there are very few suppliers; 20-year PPAs reflect seller's market
  - Supplier power: **Low-Medium** — uranium is well-supplied near-term ([[CCJ]] is primary supplier); gas supply abundant in US

## 9. Risks

1. **TMI/Crane restart execution:** $1.6B project with regulatory, engineering, and grid interconnection risk. FERC filing in April 2026 flagged potential delays. Any timeline slip undermines the bull case and the Microsoft PPA economics.
2. **Calpine integration:** Combining a nuclear-focused operator with a gas-focused fleet is operationally complex. ~$20B+ pro forma debt is elevated. Moody's revised downgrade threshold to 25% FFO/Debt. Failure to achieve >20% EPS accretion target would be a negative signal.
3. **Valuation compression:** At ~25.6x forward P/E, CEG trades at a 62% premium to IPP peers. The March 2026 selloff (-15%) on a modest guidance miss demonstrates how fragile the premium is. Any narrative shift on nuclear/AI could compress the multiple sharply.
4. **Power price / merchant exposure:** Not all revenue is contracted. Merchant nuclear economics depend on wholesale power prices; sustained low gas prices or demand softening would compress margins on un-contracted generation.
5. **AI capex cyclicality:** The nuclear-as-hyperscaler-baseload thesis depends on sustained data center buildout. If AI capex moderates or efficiency gains reduce power intensity, the PPA pipeline slows.
6. **Regulatory:** NRC license renewals (40→60→80 year extensions), state-level nuclear policy shifts, FERC Order 2023 interconnection rule changes could all impact fleet economics.
7. **Nuclear operational risk:** Fleet capacity factor of 94.7% is near maximum. Any extended outage (unplanned) at a large plant (e.g., Braidwood, Limerick) has outsized earnings impact given fixed cost structure.

## 11. Bear Case

**Scenario:** PJM regulatory ruling goes against behind-the-meter hyperscaler co-location. Two or more major hyperscaler PPAs fall through. Calpine integration disappoints with EBITDA contribution below $1.5B in first full year. Market de-rates nuclear premium.

**Implied Bear Price:** $220 (~16% downside from ~$263 current)

**Change Rating to Hold/Sell If:**
1. **Adverse PJM ruling.** FERC or PJM issues a final rule that effectively prohibits the hyperscaler co-location model that underpins Crane/TMI restart economics. Watch the PJM CIFP proceeding; any interim order that suspends pending co-location applications is the signal.
2. **Calpine EBITDA below $1.5B.** Management guided >$2B FCF accretion. If first-year Calpine contribution prints below $1.5B adj. EBITDA on the Q3 or Q4 2026 earnings call, integration risk is real.
3. **Nuclear capacity factor drops below 90% for two consecutive quarters.** CEG's entire margin thesis depends on 93-95% fleet utilization. Any sustained operational degradation is a thesis-level event.

**What Would NOT Change the Rating:** Single quarter of wind segment impairment, PJM regulatory uncertainty without final adverse ruling, or temporary delivery slippage on one turbine program.
